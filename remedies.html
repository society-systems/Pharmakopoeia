ON THE COMMON ORIGINS OF REMEDIES & MEDIA:

Or what sets the body of legal immunity

In Sanskrit, ’midiur’ denotes ’I judge, I estimate’.

In Avestan, the Indo-Iranian language that precedes Old Persian, ’vimad’ means ’healer, physician’.

In Greek, ’medos’ indicates ’to counsel, to plan’.

In today’s English lingua franca, ’accommodate’, ’empty’, ’medicine’, ’commodity’, ’medium’, ’mode’, ’modern’, ’modulation’, mood’, ’meditate’, meet’ and ’remedy’ all share the same Proto-Indo-European root: ’med-’.

The meaning of ’med-’ is standardly defined in etymology and semasiology as ’to take appropriate measures’.

fig. 1 portrays the nervous system as illustrated in Ibn Sina/Avicenna’s
’The Canon of Medicine’. This encyclopedia of medicine was written
in 1025 and translated to Latin around the middle of the 12th c.,
constituting for several centuries one of the most far-reaching references
of modern medicine. Interestingly, if we were to literally translate the
original title in Arabic, ’al-Qānūn fī al-tibb’, to English, it would be
’The Law in Medicine’. ’Qānūn’ is the Arabic word for ’law’,
derived from the Greek /kanón/ , meaning “measuring rod”.
Some of the other nominal derivatives of /kanón/ are:

  • - rods used in shields
  • - type of rod used in weaving
  • - measuring rod, carpenter’s rule, plumb line, level
  • - monochord (music)
  • - something to judge by, rule, norm, general principle
  • - model, paradigm
  • - tax assessment, tax, tariff

Law sounds so mono. It shields some and measures others. The legal practice we have at play in our world today is based on states of exceptions; legal immunity.

More to that, if we were to literally re-translate today /al-Jihāz al-’asabī/; 'nervous system’, to let’s say, English, it would be: ’the nervous device’.
